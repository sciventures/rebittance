Let's make remittances cheaper.

In 2014, an expected US$436 Billion will be remitted by immigrants back to their families in the developing world. The cost of sending all those remittances is estimated at US$42 billion or almost 10% of the entire amount.

Using Bitcoin, that number can be reduced to just US $5 billion, or 1.1%.

We call this REBITTANCE.

Every year, a huge portion of the money remitted by immigrants is lost to transfer fees and bank charges.

The Bitcoin network allows us to send funds safely and quickly, without incurring hefty fees from Western Union, Moneygram, or other remittance providers. [How?]

Imagine what the developing world could do with an extra $42 billion each year.

How Does It Work?

To send a rebittance to your family, you must first exchange your dollars or euros for Bitcoin (BTC). It's a lot like any other currency, except that it's completely digital and doesn't have a physical appearance. Unlike other currencies, its digital nature allows it to be transmitted across the world very cheaply.

  1. 1

    Sender decides how much cash they want to send back home.

  2. 2

    Sender pays for the transfer with their Bitcoin wallet.

  3. 3

    Recipient receives local cash in their bank account or mobile wallet, or picks it up personally.

Why Is It Cheaper?

Traditional remittances must move from 3 to 4 separate banking entities before finding their way to their intended recipients. At each step, certain fees are levied. Each bank must secure its respective network and facilities and this costs money.

Bitcoin does away with this by only having a single entity in between the sender and the receiver. Additionally, the Bitcoin network is secured by peers and the responsibility of maintaining it is shared amongst hundreds of thousands of users.
